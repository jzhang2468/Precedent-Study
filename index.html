Overview

Dataset archaeology as an interface problem

Excavating AI is a 2019 digital essay and research project by Kate Crawford and Trevor Paglen. Working through what the authors call an archaeology of datasets, it examines how machine learning systems such as ImageNet are assembled from labeled images, and how the taxonomies used to sort those images quietly encode assumptions, judgments, and biases about people.

The project opens up the training data that teaches AI systems how to see, arguing that these classifications are never neutral. In this redesign, the website behaves like an inspection tool: the viewer can trace how a visual input becomes a dataset item, then a category, then a social claim.

Part 2 / Layers of Analysis

Inspect the project as nested systems

This section reads Excavating AI as a composed system: a set of technical objects, cultural assumptions, historical pressures, and representational choices. The project is not only about datasets; it is itself built like an archive that asks viewers to inspect the machinery of classification.

01

Ontological analysis

At the simplest level, the project asks what exists inside machine vision before a model ever appears to see. It lays out the hidden ontology of AI training: images, labels, taxonomies, classes, annotators, interfaces, models, users, institutions, and the social meanings carried by every category.

Material layer

Photographs, scraped web images, historical archives, face datasets, screenshots, and labeled image examples become the project's basic matter. The argument begins by refusing to treat images as neutral raw material.

02

Historical and contextual analysis

The project appeared in 2019, at a moment when ImageNet was both a celebrated foundation of contemporary AI and newly vulnerable to public critique. ImageNet had been introduced in 2009, helped structure a decade of computer-vision research, and became tied to the 2012 deep-learning breakthrough that made neural-network image classification a dominant story in AI.

Crawford and Paglen were responding to the myth that machine vision is a neutral technical achievement. Their question is historical as much as technical: how did computer vision inherit older habits of sorting, measuring, naming, and judging people? The essay connects modern datasets to longer histories of classification, facial measurement, affect recognition, phrenology-like thinking, and institutional power.

The intended audience is deliberately mixed. It addresses AI researchers and technologists by examining training-set infrastructure, but it also speaks to artists, designers, theorists, students, museum publics, and general readers. The related Training Humans exhibition at Fondazione Prada extended the argument into a photography and visual-culture context, framing training images as objects that deserve historical, aesthetic, and political scrutiny.

03

Visual and aesthetic representation

Excavating AI uses a visual language of evidence. It does not rely on speculative AI imagery or futuristic abstraction. Instead, it shows the ordinary materials of machine vision: interface screenshots, taxonomy trees, archival photographs, face grids, dataset montages, labels, captions, and examples of misclassification.

This representational strategy makes the invisible infrastructure of AI visible. The aesthetic is forensic and archival: images are arranged like proof, not illustration. By preserving the look of dataset interfaces and technical documentation, the project lets the viewer feel how bureaucratic and banal classification can appear, even when its social consequences are severe.

The style sits between several communities and precedents: conceptual photography, institutional critique, media archaeology, science and technology studies, critical data visualization, and exhibition-based archival research. Its visual argument depends on a reversal: the dataset looks at people, but the project asks the audience to look back at the dataset.

Author File

What Crawford and Paglen wrote or said about the project

  1. Crawford, Kate, and Trevor Paglen. "Excavating AI: The Politics of Images in Machine Learning Training Sets." Excavating AI, AI Now Institute, New York University, 19 Sept. 2019.

    The original digital essay, where the authors describe their method as an archaeology of datasets.

  2. Crawford, Kate, and Trevor Paglen. "Excavating AI: The Politics of Images in Machine Learning Training Sets." AI & Society, vol. 36, 2021, pp. 1105-1116.

    The peer-reviewed version, which notes that an earlier version first appeared in September 2019.

  3. Paglen, Trevor. "Excavating AI." Trevor Paglen Studio, 21 Jan. 2020.

    A short author statement describing the essay's focus on the flawed assumptions and politics built into AI training data.

  4. Paglen, Trevor. "ImageNet Roulette." Trevor Paglen Studio, 29 Apr. 2020.

    Explains ImageNet Roulette as an experiment showing what goes wrong when models learn from ImageNet's person categories.

  5. Paglen, Trevor. "Training Humans." Trevor Paglen Studio, 9 Apr. 2020.

    Describes the exhibition, conceived with Crawford, on the history of images used to recognize humans in computer vision.

  6. Fondazione Prada. "Kate Crawford | Trevor Paglen: Training Humans." 2019-2020.

    Exhibition context in which Crawford frames the work as an attempt to open the black boxes of AI.

Response File

How the project circulated and was contested

  1. Simonite, Tom. "The Viral App That Labels You Isn't Quite What You Think." Wired, 2019.

    Reports how ImageNet Roulette went viral and revealed biased, problematic labels inside ImageNet.

  2. Frieze. "How AI Selfie App ImageNet Roulette Took the Internet by Storm." Frieze, 2019.

    Frames the project through art-world and internet reception, focusing on the labels users shared publicly.

  3. Wong, Julia Carrie. "The Viral Selfie App ImageNet Roulette Seemed Fun." The Guardian, 2019.

    Discusses the experience of being labeled by the tool and the racialized categories it exposed.

  4. Lyons, Michael J. "Excavating 'Excavating AI': The Elephant in the Gallery." arXiv, 2020.

    A critical response raising concerns about informed consent and how facial-image datasets were exhibited.

  5. Lyons, Michael J. "'Excavating AI' Re-excavated: Debunking a Fallacious Account of the JAFFE Dataset." arXiv, 2021.

    Continues the critique, challenging the authors' account of the JAFFE dataset.
