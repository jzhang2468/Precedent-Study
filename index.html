Part 1 / Data Sheet

What is the precedent?

Excavating AI is a digital essay, research project, and public artwork that studies how training images become political infrastructure. It moves upstream from spectacular AI outputs to the datasets, labels, taxonomies, and institutional choices that make those outputs possible.

Authors Kate Crawford + Trevor Paglen

Crawford brings AI politics, data studies, and institutional critique. Paglen brings visual investigation, surveillance studies, and photographic evidence.

Date September 19, 2019

Published by AI Now Institute, then extended through Training Humans at Fondazione Prada and the Barbican.

Medium Digital essay + dataset experiment

Text, screenshots, dataset images, archival references, ImageNet Roulette, and exhibition display.

Site of inquiry ImageNet and adjacent face datasets

ImageNet, WordNet, JAFFE, UTKFace, IBM Diversity in Faces, MS-Celeb, and other training-image archives.

Audience Researchers, artists, designers, technologists, publics

The project is written for people who build AI systems and people who are governed by them.

Central question Who decides what images mean?

The essay asks how labels and taxonomies translate social assumptions into apparently technical recognition.

Part 2 / Layers of Analysis

Ontology, context, visual language

The project is built like an archive that asks viewers to inspect the machinery of classification. These layers keep the original Part 2 analysis and make it navigable.

01

Ontological analysis

At the simplest level, the project asks what exists inside machine vision before a model ever appears to see. Its building blocks are images, labels, taxonomies, classes, annotators, interfaces, models, users, institutions, and the social meanings carried by every category.

Images are not neutral inputs. They are scraped, selected, cropped, captioned, stored, and re-used across technical and cultural contexts.

Part 3 / Method, Rhetoric, Practice

What method makes the critique possible?

Part 3 shifts from what the project is to how it works: its software approach, data provenance, theoretical method, rhetorical argument, and connection to Crawford and Paglen's wider practices.

ImageNet Roulette trained an open-source Caffe model on ImageNet's "person" categories, used face detection, and returned bounding boxes plus labels. The software was not offered as a neutral demo. It was a controlled provocation that exposed what a model learns when the dataset is already corrupted by social judgment.

The evidence comes from ImageNet, WordNet, JAFFE, UTKFace, IBM Diversity in Faces, MS-Celeb, and other image collections. The project follows where images came from, how they were annotated, who was visible or invisible in the process, and how categories traveled from language systems into computer-vision infrastructure.

The method combines media archaeology, science and technology studies, semiotics, institutional critique, critical race and gender analysis, and visual culture. Its key move is to read a dataset as a historical artifact with layers, assumptions, labor, and politics.

The precedent argues that AI classification is not simply technical. It makes the claim through shock, evidence, and reversal: first the reader sees ordinary object labels, then person categories reveal cruelty, absurdity, and inherited power. The essay is critical because it exposes the infrastructure that normally remains hidden.

For Crawford, the project continues a practice of mapping AI as material, political, and extractive, from Anatomy of an AI System to Atlas of AI and Calculating Empires. For Paglen, it extends a long investigation into hidden infrastructures, surveillance, machine vision, and the images used by systems of power.

The precedent is powerful because it changes where the viewer looks: away from polished AI outputs and toward the training-set architecture. My criticism is that its strongest public moments can risk spectacle; showing harmful labels and sensitive image histories must be handled carefully so critique does not reproduce the violence it exposes.

SOFTWARE APPROACH

The demo is a critical instrument.

The software workflow matters because it makes a normally invisible chain visible: upload, detect, classify, display, react. The interface becomes a rhetorical device, not only a technical one.

Appraisal + Criticism

Why this precedent matters for design

Excavating AI is a strong precedent for computational design because it treats a dataset as an authored spatial system. It shows that classification has architecture: entries, hierarchies, boundaries, rooms, exclusions, and points of force.

Appraisal

It gives designers a method for looking upstream.

The project teaches that critique should not wait until an AI system harms someone. Designers can inspect training data, collection protocols, labels, interfaces, benchmarks, and institutional incentives before the model becomes public infrastructure.

Criticism

It raises its own ethical problem of display.

The project depends on showing disturbing labels and controversial datasets. That exposure is necessary for accountability, but it also requires careful framing, consent awareness, and attention to whether harmed subjects are being made visible again for another audience.

Design takeaway

Make provenance part of the interface.

Future AI interfaces should not only output a category. They should reveal dataset lineage, confidence, uncertainty, category history, possible harms, and the human decisions behind the label.
